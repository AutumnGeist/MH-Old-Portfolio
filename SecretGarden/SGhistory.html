History of Herbal Medicine



Healing with herbal remedies is as old as mankind itself. Evidence of the use of plants as medicine has been documented for nearly 5000 years from various sources such as written documents, clay tablets, and monuments. Check out our timeline to see the evolution of herbal medicine.


Herbal Medicine Through The Ages

5000 BCE

Evidence of medicinal plant usage found on Sumerian clay tablet from Nagpur. It includes 12 recipes for herbal remedies and refers to over 250 plants.

2800 BCE

Pen Ts'ao, a book on Chinese herbs and medicines, is written by the Emperor Shen-nung. It contains 365 herbal formulas.

1550 BCE

The Ebers Papyrus, an Egyptian medical papyrus of herbal knowledge is written. It includes medical remedies for both physical and mental ailments.

1500 BCE

Treatments with plants are mentioned in the Vedas Indian holy books. They also include a list of sacred plants and their effects and uses.

800 BCE

Plant species used by the Minoans, Mycenaeans, Egyptians, and Assyrians for medicinal purposes are referenced in Homer's epics The Illiad and The Odyssey

400 BCE

The Greek Physician Hippocrates writes extensively on herbal medicine and how to diagnose and treat common illnesses. His works contain over 300 medicinal plants classifed by physiological action.

77 AD

A Greek Physician in the Roman Army, Dioscorides, writes De Materia Medica, which is used as a medicinal plant reference through the middle ages.

200 AD

Herbal medicine becomes popular throughout the Roman Empire. Galen, the most distinguised Roman physician, publishes works on medicinal plants and their effects.

1000s AD

Arabian healers gain renown and begin to influence medical practices. The Canon of Medicine is written by Avicenna, which sets the standards for medicine in Medieval Europe and the Islamic world.

1300s AD

The Black Death sweeps across Europe. Herbal treatments are proven to be just as effective, if not more, than popular treatments such as blood letting and purging.

1500s AD

King Henry VII of England and his Parliament encourage and promote trained herbalists to improve the standard of care.

1700s AD

Methodist preacher Charles Wesley advocates for clean living and promotes herbal treatments for good health. Rich patients can afford drugs, but the poor are treated with herbal remedies.

1800s AD

Turning point in the knowledge and use of medicinal plants. The discovery and isolation of plant alkaloids marks the beginning of scientific pharmacy. The National Institue of Medical Herbalists is founded in England 1864.

1900s AD

Drug shortages during the World Wars lead to a renewed interest in herbal medicine. Stabilization methods for fresh medicinal plants are researched.

2000-Present

The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that about 80% of the poor populations in countries like Africa and Asia, use herbal medicine as their primary health care. Approximately 25% of modern drugs used in the United States are derived from plants.
